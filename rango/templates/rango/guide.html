Lifting Guides

The Squat

The squat is often called the "king of all lifts," and for good reason. It is not just a test of leg strength, but a full-body expression of stability, coordination, and power. A heavy squat demands precision in both setup and execution to move weight efficiently and safely.

The Foundation: Bracing and Positioning

Before the descent even begins, the lift is won or lost in the setup. A strong squat starts with proper bracing—filling the abdomen with air and creating intra-abdominal pressure to stabilize the spine. This turns your torso into a rigid pillar capable of supporting heavy loads.

Your feet should be planted firmly, typically shoulder-width apart, with toes slightly pointed out to allow the hips to open naturally. The bar must be secured tightly across the upper back, with the shoulder blades pulled together to create a muscular “shelf.”

The Descent and Drive

The squat is initiated by breaking at the hips and knees simultaneously, sitting “down and back” while maintaining a neutral spine. Depth is key—reaching at least parallel ensures full muscle engagement and competition-standard form.

On the ascent, the focus shifts to driving through the midfoot while keeping the chest up and knees tracking over the toes. The hips and shoulders should rise together—any collapse forward indicates a loss of tightness or positioning.

Efficiency in the squat comes from maintaining balance and bar path over the midfoot, minimizing wasted motion and maximizing force production.

The Bench Press

The bench press is often dismissed as a simple "ego lift," but for the serious strength athlete, it is a complex, total-body maneuver. To move significant weight safely, you must master the art of the setup and the science of the bar path.

The Foundation: Setup and Leg Drive

Strength begins on the floor. Proper leg drive creates a stable platform and allows you to transfer force from the ground, through your core, and into the bar. Your shoulder blades must be retracted and depressed—tucked into your back pockets—to create a stable base and protect your rotator cuffs.

A slight arch in the lower back is not only safe but recommended to maintain thoracic extension and optimize the angle of the chest fibers. This isn't cheating; it's structural engineering for the human body.

The Bar Path

A straight vertical line is rarely the most efficient path for a heavy bench press. The J-curve, where the bar descends toward the lower sternum and ascends back toward the face, allows for optimal leverage and reduces the mechanical disadvantage at the weakest point of the lift.

The Deadlift

The deadlift is the purest demonstration of raw strength—lifting a weight from the ground to lockout with no assistance from momentum. Despite its apparent simplicity, it is a highly technical lift where small errors can significantly impact performance and safety.

The Foundation: Setup and Tension

A strong deadlift begins with a disciplined setup. The bar should start over the midfoot, with the shins close but not pushing it forward. Grip the bar firmly and engage the lats—think of “bending the bar” toward your body—to keep it close throughout the lift.

Before initiating the pull, tension must be created through the entire body. This means pulling the slack out of the bar, bracing the core, and setting the back in a neutral, locked-in position. The body should feel like a coiled spring, ready to release force.

The Pull and Lockout

The deadlift is a push, not just a pull. Drive through the floor with your legs while maintaining a constant back angle as the bar leaves the ground. The bar should travel in a straight vertical line, staying as close to the body as possible to reduce unnecessary strain.

As the bar passes the knees, the hips drive forward aggressively to meet the bar, finishing in a strong lockout with the chest tall and glutes fully engaged. Leaning back excessively at the top is unnecessary and can place undue stress on the lower back.

Mastery of the deadlift lies in patience off the floor and precision in movement—maximizing efficiency while minimizing risk.
